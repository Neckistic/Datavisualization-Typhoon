The Big Picture

Between 1988 and 2023, Hong Kong was affected by 207 tropical cyclones. These storms brought destructive winds, torrential rain, dangerous storm surges, and billions of dollars in economic losses. Where Do the Storms Go?

Each cyclone icon marks the closest point of approach of a tropical cyclone relative to the Hong Kong Observatory. Colours now map to individual warning levels, letting you compare Signal 1, 3, 8, 9, and 10 storms separately. The icon size reflects economic loss, while the 100 km, 400 km, and 800 km rings help show how close each storm came to Hong Kong.

Switch to Avg Wind or Avg Rainfall view to see how weather stations across Hong Kong experience different average conditions — coastal stations like Waglan Island and Cheung Chau consistently record the strongest gusts, while mountainous areas catch the heaviest rain.

Key insight: Storms approaching from the south and southwest tend to cause the highest surges because they push water directly into Victoria Harbour.

How Often Do Typhoons Strike?

This stacked bar chart shows how many tropical cyclones affected Hong Kong each year, broken down by signal level. Most years see 4–8 events, but the severity mix varies widely. Some years (like 1993 and 1999) saw clusters of strong storms, while others passed relatively quietly.

What to look for: Notice how the proportion of red and orange bars (higher signals) is not increasing over time — while climate change is expected to intensify individual storms, it hasn't yet produced a clear upward trend in the frequency of severe warnings in Hong Kong.

Comparing Impact Dimensions

Not all typhoons are created equal. The radar chart compares the average profile of storms at each signal level across five dimensions: wind gust, rainfall, storm surge, economic loss, and people affected. Higher-signal storms clearly dominate across every axis — and the jump from Signal 8 to Signal 9 and 10 is especially visible in loss and affected counts.

The box plot shows how economic losses are distributed within each signal level. The wide range of outliers for Signal 8 reflects the fact that many factors beyond wind speed — rainfall, timing, storm surge, and track — determine the final damage.

Key insight: Signal 9 and Signal 10 storms sit in a very different loss range from Signal 8 storms. The jump from Signal 8 upward marks the point where damage becomes far more likely to turn catastrophic.

The Economic Toll — A Rising Trend?

Each bubble represents a cyclone that caused measurable economic loss. The vertical axis uses a logarithmic scale — so each grid line represents a 10× increase in damage. Bubble size reflects the number of people affected, and colour indicates the signal level.

The most striking feature is Super Typhoon Mangkhut (2018), sitting at the very top with HK$4.6 billion in estimated damages. But notice how recent years (2019–2023) have several high-loss events — suggesting that even moderate storms now cause more economic disruption as Hong Kong's built environment grows denser and more valuable.

What to look for: Hover over any bubble to see detailed storm information. The labelled names help you identify the most notorious storms. Note how Typhoon York (1999), Typhoon Vicente (2012), and Typhoon Hato (2017) each represented the worst event of their respective decades.

The Human Cost

Behind every statistic is a human story. This chart tracks deaths (red bars), injuries (orange bars), and people affected (blue line, scaled ÷10) across the 35-year period. The good news: fatalities have generally declined, reflecting improved forecasting, better building codes, and an effective warning system.

However, the number of people affected has risen sharply in recent events like Mangkhut (2018) and Haikui (2023), as population density increases and more infrastructure is exposed to storm surges and flooding.

Key insight: While deaths have declined from an average of ~5/year in the 1990s to near zero in recent years, the affected population has surged — showing that Hong Kong is better at saving lives but increasingly vulnerable to disruption.

Understanding the Warning System

Hong Kong's tropical cyclone warning system is one of the most recognised in Asia. Signals range from 1 (standby) to 10 (hurricane-force winds), and each level triggers specific actions across the city — from school closures to full transport shutdowns.

Signal 1 (Standby) – A tropical cyclone is within 800 km of Hong Kong. Stay alert and monitor updates.

Signal 3 (Strong Wind) – Sustained winds of 41–62 km/h expected. Secure loose outdoor items.

Signal 8 (Gale or Storm) – Winds 63–117 km/h. Schools and offices close. All should stay indoors.

Signal 9 (Increasing Gale) – Significant intensification imminent. Extreme danger outdoors.

Signal 10 (Hurricane) – Sustained winds ≥118 km/h. Maximum destructive force. Most severe warning in HK.

Did you know? Super Typhoon Mangkhut (2018) caused an estimated HK$4.6 billion in damage — the costliest tropical cyclone in Hong Kong's modern history. Signal 10 was hoisted, and storm surge reached 2.35m above normal.
